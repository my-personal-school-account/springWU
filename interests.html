My interests

    This page is a relatively broad description of my different interests and will contain both text and images describing them.     Climbing

    There's a particular kind of focus that climbing demands — one that drowns out everything else. The moment you're on the wall, the only things that exist are your hands, your feet, and the next move. It's equal parts physical and mental, a puzzle that constantly reinvents itself. I got into climbing a few years ago and it quickly took over my life in the best way possible. What started as a casual gym session turned into weekend trips to outdoor crags, obsessive research into gear, and a growing tick-list of routes I want to climb before I die.

    Bouldering scratches a different itch than sport climbing — shorter, more explosive problems that you can work on for hours until something finally clicks. That moment when a sequence that felt impossible suddenly flows perfectly is one of the best feelings in the world. The climbing community is also something special. It's one of those rare spaces where complete strangers will spend an hour coaching you through a problem, cheering you on like they've known you for years. I've made some of my closest friends on a wall.

    Nature

    Long before I had the vocabulary for it, nature was where I went to feel okay. Something about being outside — really outside, away from roads and notifications and artificial light — resets something deep in the brain that nothing else quite touches. I grew up near the countryside and spent a lot of my childhood just wandering, which I think gave me a lifelong appetite for open space. These days that manifests as weekend hikes, camping trips, and a genuine excitement about weather that most people find baffling.

    There's something to be said for learning to read a landscape — noticing the way light changes through trees at different times of day, recognising birds by their calls, understanding why a trail cuts a certain way across a hillside. The more you pay attention, the more there is to see. I try to do at least one longer trip a year — somewhere with proper wilderness, somewhere that takes actual effort to reach. The difficulty is half the point. There's a satisfaction in being somewhere genuinely remote that a short Sunday stroll can't replicate. That said, I've also learned to love the small doses. A lunch break in a park, a detour through some woods on the way home, noticing the seasons change in a tree outside a window. Nature doesn't always have to be a grand expedition.

    Computers

    Computers I've been taking things apart to see how they work for as long as I can remember, and computers were the natural extension of that. There's something endlessly satisfying about a machine that does exactly what you tell it to — and endlessly humbling about how often you realise you don't quite know what you're telling it. My interest spans a pretty wide range — from low-level stuff like hardware and networking, to writing code, to just being genuinely excited about where the field is heading. I find it hard to stay in one lane, which means I know a lot about a lot and an expert-level amount about very little, which seems fine.

    Programming in particular has been a huge part of my life. There's a creative satisfaction to building something from scratch that I find hard to compare to anything else. It's one of the few things where the gap between imagination and execution is mostly just time and effort. I'm also just a bit of a hardware nerd. There's something deeply pleasurable about a well-built machine — the click of a good keyboard, a monitor with excellent colour accuracy, a setup that's been tuned just right. It's a rabbit hole I've fallen down more than once.

    Cats

    I didn't choose to be a cat person. It chose me. And I've never once regretted it. Cats occupy a very specific niche in the animal kingdom: creatures that are entirely capable of surviving without you, but choose — on their own terms, in their own time — to stick around anyway. There's something about that conditional affection that makes it feel genuinely earned.

    I love the way cats have completely distinct personalities. Some are chaotic, some are serene, some are deeply weird in ways that are difficult to articulate. They're also, objectively, extremely funny. The commitment with which a cat will knock something off a shelf and then make direct eye contact with you as it falls is a level of audacity I can only aspire to. They're also quietly good for your mental health in ways people don't always talk about. The routine of caring for another creature, the warmth of something alive settling next to you, the white noise of a purr — it all adds up. Science backs this up, for what it's worth, but honestly you don't need a study. You just need to sit with a cat for twenty minutes. Whether I end up with one cat or six, I think I've made peace with the fact that this is just who I am now.
